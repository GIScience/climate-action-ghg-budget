# CO₂ budget calculation

A CO₂ budget shows how much carbon dioxide (CO₂) a city or country is still permitted to emit in order to limit global
warming to a specific temperature.
The Paris Agreement, adopted in 2015, is particularly important in this regard.
Under this agreement, many countries have committed to keeping global warming well below 2 °C compared to pre-industrial
levels.
Where possible, warming should even be limited to 1.5 °C.

To ensure these targets are met, every country and every city has a responsibility.
This is why the selected city must also reduce its CO₂ emissions.
To this end, a calculation is made of how much CO₂ the selected city is still permitted to emit without further
worsening global warming.
This amount is called the CO₂ budget.

The global budget specifies how much CO₂ may still be emitted worldwide in order to stay within the limit of 1.5 °C or 2
°C of global warming.
To calculate the CO₂ budget of the selected city, we take the global CO₂ budget and allocate it according to the city's
population share of the world population.
This gives us the CO₂ budget that would be fair for the selected city.

The cities for which the CO₂ budget tool is available measure their emissions according to a specific standard,
the [BISKO framework](https://www.kea-bw.de/fileadmin/user_upload/Energiemanagement/Angebote/Beschreibung_der_BISKO-Methodik.pdf).
This estimate shows how much CO₂ is generated in the selected city.
However, BISKO only records emissions that arise directly within the city, such as those from cars or heating systems.
It does not account for emissions caused by residents of the city outside the city limits, for example through air
travel or products sourced from other regions.
As a result, the BISKO data covers only approximately 64 % of the total CO₂ emissions actually caused by residents of
the selected city.
The remaining 36 % are emissions generated outside the city limits that must nevertheless be attributed to the city's
population.
The budget displayed shows only the 64 % of emissions according to the BISKO standard.
