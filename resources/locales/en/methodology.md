# CO₂-Budget calculation

A so-called CO₂ budget refers to the amount of CO₂ that may be emitted in order to limit global warming to a specific
temperature (e.g. 1.5 °C).
As such, these budgets form the basis for meeting the goals set by the Paris Agreement.
The Paris Agreement was adopted at the end of 2015 during the UN Climate Conference in Paris.
In this agreement, nations commit to limiting global warming to "well below" 2 °C compared to pre-industrial levels and
to making efforts to limit it to 1.5 °C.
This means that every country and its population is assigned responsibility for addressing their share of the global
problem.
This is quantified through CO₂ budgets.

This tool shows the remaining CO₂ budget that the selected city may still emit in order to make its fair contribution to
limiting global warming to a specific temperature (1.5 °C, 1.7 °C, or 2 °C).
To determine this, we first calculate the global CO₂ budget available to us to limit warming to the temperatures
mentioned above.
As the Paris Climate Agreement was adopted at the end of 2015, we refer to the CO₂ budget from 2016 onwards.
However, we use figures for the global CO₂ budget from
the [2021 IPCC report (Table SPM.2, p. 29)](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/downloads/report/IPCC_AR6_WGI_SPM.pdf).
This report specifies the global budget from 2020 onwards.
To still calculate the budget from 2016 onwards, we add the global CO₂ emissions between 2016 and 2020 to the global
budget of 2020.
The global CO₂ budget for the various temperature limits is then scaled down to the selected city's share of the world
population.
This urban budget encompasses all CO₂ emissions caused by the local population.

We compare the scaled-down budget with the actually measured emissions.
The cities for which the CO₂ budget tool is available measure their emissions according to
the [BISKO methodology](https://www.kea-bw.de/fileadmin/user_upload/Energiemanagement/Angebote/Beschreibung_der_BISKO-Methodik.pdf).
The CO₂ accounting for Heidelberg until 2018 shows that the so-called BISKO emissions cover only around 64 % of the
total emissions caused by the Heidelberg's population.
The reason for this incomplete coverage is that, put simply, the BISKO standard only takes into account emissions
generated within the city limits, but not those caused by the city's population outside those boundaries.
As we use the emission values determined by the cities according to the BISKO methodology, we must also take a nuanced
view of the CO₂ budget for the selected city .
A part of the budget, in the
case of Heidelberg precisely 64 %, can be allocated to BISKO emissions.
The remainder is reserved for non-BISKO emissions.
According to this reasoning, and assuming that the share of BISKO emissions in total emissions is similar across all
available cities, the final CO₂ budget represents only 64 % of the total budget of the local urban population.

# Data basis

The calculation of the CO₂-budget are based on data on the global CO₂-budget by the IPCC.
